Robot Authority and Post-Error Recovery in Human-Robot Interaction




ABSTRACT
As social robots enter shared human environments, questions about how their perceived social roles shape human behavior become increasingly important. This paper investigates whether a robot framed as an authority figure amplifies the psychological cost of human failure compared to the same robot framed as an assistant. Drawing on the Computers Are Social Actors (CASA) paradigm, social facilitation theory, and research on hierarchical power, we predict that participants who interact with a Manager robot will show longer post-error recovery times and report higher stress after making mistakes than participants who interact with an Assistant robot. To test this, we use a virtual escape room as our task environment, where participants work through trial-and-error puzzles that tend to produce failures without scripting them. The robot's informational content is held constant across conditions and only the social framing changes.

1 INTRODUCTION
As robots move out of factories and into shared social environments, a new set of questions emerges around perceived social roles and how they shape human psychology. It is well established that robots can influence whether people comply, trust, or disengage [4, 7, 15]. What we know far less about is what happens inside the interaction at a finer grain, specifically in the moments after a person makes a mistake. This paper asks whether a robot framed as an authority figure makes those moments worse.
The foundation for this inquiry is the Computers Are Social Actors (CASA) paradigm, which established that humans apply the same social scripts to machines that they apply to other people, often without realizing it [13]. If robots inherit our social expectations, they also inherit our social anxieties. Classical social facilitation research showed that being observed by someone with evaluative power is enough to degrade human performance on complex tasks, not because of distraction but because of the psychological weight of being judged [1, 3, 17]. That weight does not disappear just because the observer is a machine. Riether et al. [14] confirmed that robot observers can trigger the same evaluative threat response in human participants that human observers do.
The existing human-robot interaction (HRI) literature has explored robot authority mostly through compliance and overall task performance: will people follow a robot's instructions, and does having a robot lead a team improve or hurt efficiency [7]? These are important questions, but they miss something. The moment right after a failure, when a person has just visibly made a mistake in front of an evaluating authority, is where we think the psychological pressure of robot social power is felt most acutely. That hesitation before trying again is what we set out to measure.
To test this, we use a virtual escape room set in the University of Chicago's Regenstein Library as our task environment. Participants work through a series of logic and pattern-matching puzzles while interacting with a robot framed either as a Manager or an Assistant. The puzzles are designed to require some trial and error, so failures tend to occur without needing to be scripted in. The robot's informational content is held constant across conditions and only the social framing changes.
We predict that participants in the Manager condition will wait significantly longer before attempting the next puzzle after a failure, because the evaluative threat created by a supervisory robot raises the psychological cost of being seen to fail again (H1). We also predict they will report higher stress and discomfort on self-report measures (H2). If we are right, it means robot social power does not just shape compliance or trust at the macro level but gets into the texture of the interaction itself.
2 BACKGROUND
2.1 The CASA Paradigm
When Reeves and Nass [13] proposed the CASA paradigm, the provocative claim was that people treat computers like people, not metaphorically but psychologically. Decades of subsequent research have supported that claim. Humans attribute warmth, competence, and dominance to robots, and those attributions produce real behavioral consequences [2]. A robot that acts like a peer gets treated like a peer. A robot that acts like a supervisor gets treated like one too.
Nass and Moon [10] demonstrated that the effect holds even when participants are explicitly told they are interacting with a computer and that social responses are inappropriate. Their work showed that social responses to computers are largely automatic, triggered by cues in the interface rather than deliberate decisions. This automaticity is central to our hypothesis: participants do not need to consciously decide to treat the Manager robot as a real authority figure for the evaluative pressure to affect their behavior.
Research has further shown that different robot morphologies and behavioral styles modulate these social response effects [2, 8]. Humanoid robots tend to elicit stronger social responses, but even non-humanoid robots with socially meaningful behaviors produce measurable attribution of social roles. This work supports the premise that social framing manipulations, such as labeling a robot a Manager versus an Assistant, should be sufficient to activate different social scripts in participants.
2.2 Social Facilitation and Evaluative Threat
Zajonc [17] established that the presence of others enhances performance on simple, well-learned tasks but impairs performance on complex or novel ones. The mechanism was later clarified by Cottrell et al. [3], who argued that it is not mere presence but evaluative apprehension, the belief that one is being judged, that drives performance effects. When someone with power over you is watching, cognitive resources get diverted toward self-monitoring and threat management rather than the task at hand.
The behavioral signature of evaluative threat is well documented: hesitation, risk aversion, and reduced willingness to re-engage after a mistake [1]. Bond and Titus [1] conducted an influential meta-analysis of 241 social facilitation studies and confirmed that the presence of an evaluative audience reliably increases arousal and impairs complex task performance. The effect is especially pronounced in the period immediately following an observable failure, when the psychological salience of being evaluated is highest.
Dickerson and Kemeny [5] extended this line of research to show that social-evaluative threat produces both subjective stress and measurable physiological responses. Their meta-analysis of 208 laboratory studies found that tasks where participants could be negatively judged by others produced substantially larger stress responses than tasks without that evaluative component. This provides a theoretical basis for expecting that our Manager condition will produce not just different behavior but different subjective experience, which we assess through the NASA-TLX and RoSAS.
2.3 Robot Authority in HRI
Social power is one of the most consequential role dimensions in any interaction. In human hierarchies, evaluative authority from a high-power figure produces heightened self-monitoring, increased anxiety, and greater sensitivity to failure [6]. HRI research has examined these dynamics mostly at the level of whether humans comply with robot instructions [7] or follow a robot's lead in an emergency [15]. The specific question of how authority shapes the experience of human error has not been directly addressed.
Gombolay et al. [7] showed that human-robot teams perform better when the robot takes on a scheduling and coordination role traditionally associated with authority, and participants reported greater satisfaction when the robot took on more authority. This suggests that authority framing affects subjective experience, not just compliance behavior. However, their work did not examine failure conditions or post-error dynamics.
Robinette et al. [15] found that people will follow a robot's guidance even in emergency situations, even when they have reason to question the robot's competence. All 26 participants in their study followed a robot during a simulated fire emergency, despite half having just watched the same robot perform poorly on a navigation task. Our work complements this by asking not whether people comply with robot authority but how robot authority shapes the psychological experience of the interaction when the human makes an error.
Fiske [6] provides important theoretical grounding here. In her account of social power, high-power others receive less careful attention from those below them in a hierarchy, while lower-power individuals attend carefully to those with power over them and are highly sensitive to their evaluations. This asymmetry is precisely what our Manager versus Assistant manipulation is designed to activate: participants in the Manager condition should experience the robot as an evaluating authority, increasing their sensitivity to the robot's perceived judgment of their mistakes.
2.4 Post-Error Behavior and Recovery
Research on post-error adjustment has established that people reliably slow down following mistakes in cognitive tasks, a phenomenon known as post-error slowing [12]. Rabbitt [12] first described this effect, and it has since been replicated across a wide range of task domains. Post-error slowing is thought to reflect both a corrective cognitive process and a motivational response to failure. Notebaert et al. [11] proposed that post-error slowing is partly driven by the orienting response to the surprising event of making a mistake, rather than a deliberate strategic adjustment.
Moser et al. [9] showed that individual differences in how people process errors predict behavioral post-error adjustment, and that mindset and evaluative context shape how much someone slows down after a mistake. This suggests that the conditions under which an error occurs, not just the error itself, influence recovery behavior. We expect the evaluative pressure of the Manager condition to function as one such context, making the sting of failure feel larger and producing longer recovery times.
We measure post-error recovery time as our primary behavioral outcome, defined as the elapsed time between a failed puzzle attempt and the participant's next input. This captures the hesitation we are interested in within a task context where failure happens organically rather than on demand.
2.5 Task Paradigm
We use a virtual escape room as our task environment because the puzzle structure tends to produce failures through trial and error, without requiring us to script errors or instruct participants to make mistakes deliberately. Zhao et al. [18] similarly used an escape room setting to study user emotions and robot perception in HRI, and found that the goal-directed structure of the task produced engaged behavior. Our primary measure is how long participants wait between a failed attempt and their next one, which we use as a behavioral indicator of hesitation under evaluative pressure.
